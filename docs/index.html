Shorthand-Compressed Text for Smaller, Faster Language Models

shoegazerstella

Independent · github.com/shoegazerstella/glyph-lm

Submitted · 2026-07-05 Corpus · tiny_shakespeare Vocab · BPE 2048 Params · ~4M Hardware · Apple M5 / MPS

Keywords stenography, tokenization, byte-pair encoding, small language models, text compression, Plover

Abstract

Stenographers compress speech to a few keystrokes per word using real chord-based systems such as Gregg, Pitman, and modern machine steno. We ask whether compressing written text the same way — before it reaches a language model’s tokenizer — yields smaller, faster, and equally capable models at a fixed vocabulary budget. Two identical tiny GPT-2 models (4 layers, n_embd=128, 4 heads, ≈4M parameters) were trained on tiny_shakespeare under identical hyperparameters and a 2048-token BPE budget. One model saw raw text; the other saw text in which the 150 most frequent words and 30 most frequent phrases were replaced with their real Plover steno chords, registered as atomic special tokens. On a held-out validation split, the shorthand model achieved lower final loss (5.81 vs 6.00), lower perplexity (286 vs 393), and higher inference throughput (415 vs 257 tokens/sec). The whitespace-token ratio dropped below 1.0 (0.975) for the first time across three iterations — evidence of genuine phrase-level compression rather than character-level relabeling.

§1 Introduction

Human stenographers reach speeds upwards of 240 words per minute by chord-compressing language: a single key combination — a chord — spells an entire word or phrase. Systems like Gregg and Pitman, and modern machine-steno engines such as Plover, encode English into a small alphabet of strokes. The strokes are arbitrary-looking strings to a lay reader — -T for the, SP for and, TPOT for not — but they are systematic, frequent, and human-interpretable.

Language models, in turn, spend most of their parameters and compute on the long tail of tokens produced by a subword tokenizer. If the most frequent spans of text are pre-compressed into short atomic symbols before tokenization, a fixed training budget should in principle cover more original text. Whether that gain is real, or whether it is silently eaten by the vocabulary budget that a BPE tokenizer has to spend, is the question this experiment isolates.

§2 Hypothesis & Method

At an equal token budget, a model trained on shorthand-compressed text should achieve a better tokens-per-character compression ratio, comparable or better perplexity, and faster inference than an identical model trained on raw text. The contrast that matters is not just the raw gap but its shape: any regression should tell us whether shorthand substitution is a fixed vocab-budget artifact (novel symbols losing BPE merge slots to common English morphemes) or a structural problem with the substitution itself.

The pipeline holds every variable constant except the input representation. A frequency-ranked subset of Plover’s real chord dictionary (top 150 words, top 30 phrases by corpus frequency, out of 4,956 word- and 703 phrase-chords available in tiny_shakespeare) is fitted against the training split and substituted via a single-pass regex. Two independent BPE tokenizers, each with vocab_size=2048, are then trained — one per corpus. Steno chords are registered as special tokens, so they occupy reserved vocab slots rather than competing for merges. Finally, two architecturally identical GPT-2 models (n_layer=4, n_head=4, n_positions=256) are trained for 3 epochs with AdamW at lr=3e-4, with MPS/CUDA/CPU auto-detection.

§3 Results

Table 1 reports the four headline metrics on the held-out validation split for the latest run; Figure 1 visualizes the same numbers as small multiples. The shorthand model wins on loss, perplexity, and inference throughput simultaneously — the first iteration in which it does so on every axis. The compression ratio (BPE tokens per source character) regresses slightly (0.352 vs 0.333), a gap narrowed by more than half relative to the prior hand-picked encoder (0.454).

§4 Discussion

The most diagnostic number in Table 1 is not the perplexity win but the whitespace-token ratio: 0.975. It is the only metric in the table that does not depend on either model’s own tokenizer, since it counts words by a plain whitespace split on the encoded corpus relative to the raw. Dropping below 1.0 means the encoder is genuinely fusing words via phrase-chording (-FT → of the, O*EUPL → I am), not merely relabeling characters. Figure 2 traces this metric across the three encoder iterations; the crossing below 1.0 coincides with the move from hand-picked phrase rules to real, frequency-ranked Plover chords.

The throughline across all three runs is that the vocabulary budget is the real constraint, not the idea of shorthand compression itself. Run 2’s compression regression and Run 3’s recovery are both consistent with the artifact framing stated in §2: reserving vocab slots for guessed symbols starves BPE of merge budget, while reserving them for frequency-ranked symbols pays back. Shorthand substitution is not structurally unworkable — it is sensitive to which symbols get the reserved slots.

§5 Limitations

This is a smoke test, not a converged experiment. Conclusions concern the mechanism, not the asymptotic quality gap. Three caveats, in order of severity:

i. Every run logged here is a ≈100-step, ≈11-second run on a 1 MB corpus; neither model’s loss has plateaued. ii. Perplexity is compared per-token across two different tokenizations, which is not strictly apples-to-apples — a shorthand token can carry more original information than a raw token. The correct normalization is bits per original character (total cross-entropy in bits ÷ length of the uncompressed reference), not yet implemented. iii. Single corpus, single seed, single vocab size (2048); a vocab-size sweep would show whether the residual compression gap closes, stays flat, or grows.
